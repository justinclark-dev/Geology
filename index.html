Convergent

Divergent

Divergent boundaries are places where plates move away from each other

Transform

Transform (shear) boundaries are places where plates slide past each other

Subduction Zones

Continental Arc

The descending oceanic plate starts to melt at about 100 km, and the magma produced there rises to form a chain of volcanoes behind the subduction zone. When this happens where oceanic lithosphere is subducted beneath a continent, the result is a chain of volcanoes inland from the trench, where the descending plate reaches the required depth. This is called a “continental arc”. The Cascade volcanoes are an excellent example of this, as are the Andes, or the Aleutian Volcanoes.

Volcanic Island Arc

When this happens where one oceanic plate is subducted beneath another, the chain of volcanic islands develops behind the trench. The Philippines, the Marianas, and an array of others in the southwest Pacific are good examples of this. These are called “island-arc” volcanoes.

Spreading Center and Subduction Zones

Wilson Cycle

The cycle of opening ocean basins with rifting and seafloor spreading, then closing the basin via subduction and collision, creating a supercontinent.

Wilson Cycle History

There have been at least four cycles over the last two billion years, and the Atlantic Basin (the youngest, with the passive margins) was born in the breakup of the last supercontinent (Pangea) 200 million years ago.
